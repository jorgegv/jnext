## 2. Current State of Affairs

### 2.1 US Copyright Office Position

The US Copyright Office has established through multiple decisions and a formal report (Part 2, published January 29, 2025) that:

- **Human authorship is required** for copyright protection. Works generated solely by AI, without meaningful human creative input, are not eligible for copyright.
- **AI-assisted works can be copyrighted**, but only to the extent that a human author has contributed sufficient creative expression. The mere provision of prompts to an AI is generally not considered sufficient creative input.
- The use of AI to assist in creation, or the inclusion of AI-generated material in a larger human-generated work, does **not automatically bar** copyrightability.
- The Office recommends documenting human involvement in AI-assisted development.

### 2.2 Key Court Rulings

#### Thaler v. Perlmutter (Settled Law as of March 2026)

The landmark US case on AI authorship:

- **District Court (August 2023):** Ruled that AI cannot be an author under the Copyright Act.
- **D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals (March 2025):** Affirmed, stating that human authorship is a "bedrock requirement." Importantly, the court clarified: *"The human authorship requirement does not prohibit copyrighting work that was made by or with the assistance of artificial intelligence."*
- **US Supreme Court (March 2, 2026):** Denied certiorari, making this effectively settled law. AI cannot be an author, but humans using AI can be authors of the resulting works, depending on their level of creative involvement.

#### GEMA v. OpenAI (Munich, Germany — November 2025)

A European landmark:

- The Munich Regional Court ruled that OpenAI's GPT models **infringed copyright** by memorizing and reproducing song lyrics almost verbatim.
- The court held that **memorization of training data constitutes reproduction** under EU/German copyright law.
- The court rejected OpenAI's text and data mining (TDM) defense.
- **This case is under appeal** and may reach the Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU).
- **Significance for JNEXT:** This ruling establishes (in at least one EU jurisdiction) that if an AI model reproduces copyrighted content from its training data, that constitutes infringement. However, this ruling concerned verbatim reproduction of song lyrics — a very different scenario from generating emulator code.

#### Doe v. GitHub / GitHub Copilot Lawsuit (Ongoing)

- Filed November 2022 against GitHub, Microsoft, and OpenAI, alleging that Copilot reproduces open-source code without proper attribution.
- Most claims were dismissed. A narrow set of breach-of-contract claims remain.
- **As of early 2026, the lawsuit remains unresolved** and has not produced a definitive ruling on whether AI-generated code constitutes a derivative work of training data.

### 2.3 EU Legal Framework

#### EU AI Act

- Article 50 transparency obligations will apply from **August 2026**.
- Requires labeling of AI-generated content: fully AI-generated vs. AI-assisted content.
- Spain's Agency for the Supervision of AI (AESIA) has published 16 guidance documents.

#### European Parliament Copyright and AI Report (2026)

- Proposed enhanced licensing and remuneration for training data use.
- Broad territorial application: EU copyright law should apply to AI models placed on the EU market, regardless of where training occurred.
- Not yet law but signals EU policy direction.

#### Spain-Specific Considerations

- Spanish copyright law (Ley de Propiedad Intelectual, Real Decreto Legislativo 1/1996) requires human authorship, consistent with EU position.
- AESIA is actively publishing AI compliance guidance.
- No Spain-specific court ruling on AI-generated code copyright exists yet.

### 2.4 Open Source Community Positions

#### Free Software Foundation (FSF)

- Expressed concern that LLMs trained on GPL-licensed code could inadvertently violate copyleft provisions.
- No definitive policy statement on whether developers can GPL-license AI-generated code.

#### Linux Kernel Policy

The most detailed policy of any major project:

- **AI agents MUST NOT add Signed-off-by tags.** Only humans certify the DCO.
- Contributions assisted by AI must include an `Assisted-by:` tag.
- The human developer bears **full responsibility** for reviewing AI-generated code.
- Linus Torvalds has stated that AI tools should be treated no differently than traditional coding aids.

#### Other Projects

- **QEMU and Gentoo** have banned AI-generated contributions.
- **Debian** debated but reached no formal decision.
- No universal community consensus exists.

### 2.5 The "Chardet" License Laundering Incident (March 2026)

A highly relevant recent case:

- The maintainer of `chardet` used Claude to rewrite the entire codebase and changed the license from LGPL to MIT, claiming a "clean room implementation."
- The open-source community widely criticized this as **"license laundering"**.
- **Key distinction from JNEXT:** The chardet case involved deliberately using AI to rewrite an existing codebase to circumvent its license. JNEXT is a new, original application — not a rewrite of an existing emulator to change its license. The FUSE Z80 core is properly vendored and attributed under its original GPLv2-or-later license, not rewritten to evade it.

## 3. Detailed Analysis

### 3.1 The Copyright Paradox for AI-Generated Code

There is a fundamental tension:

1. **To license code under GPL-3.0, you need to hold copyright over it.** The GPL is a copyright license.
2. **AI-generated code may not be copyrightable** if the human's contribution was insufficient.
3. **If the code is in the public domain, you cannot effectively enforce copyleft** — anyone could extract those portions and use them without GPL restrictions.

However, the reality is nuanced, and **your situation is particularly strong:**

- You directed the development of a complex, architecturally sophisticated emulator with multiple subsystems (CPU, memory, video pipeline, audio engine, I/O dispatch, peripheral modules, Qt GUI, debugger).
- You made creative and technical decisions at every level: choosing the accuracy model (line-accurate hybrid), designing the module architecture, selecting which VHDL signals to model, deciding layer compositing order, choosing data structures and algorithms.
- You iteratively reviewed, tested, and refined output — repeatedly sending code back for correction against the authoritative VHDL specification.
- You integrated AI output with existing copyrighted code (FUSE Z80) requiring careful interface design.
- You maintained a detailed design plan (`EMULATOR-DESIGN-PLAN.md`) that demonstrates sustained creative direction across 9 development phases.
- The git history itself is evidence of iterative human-directed development.

This level of involvement goes well beyond "typing a prompt and accepting the output." It is closer to an architect directing a construction team — the creative vision and technical decisions are yours.

### 3.2 The Contamination Risk — Specific to JNEXT

The "contamination" argument has two forms, but both carry **lower risk for an emulator project** than for general-purpose software:

**a) Verbatim reproduction risk: LOW**

- JNEXT implements ZX Spectrum Next hardware behavior based on VHDL source files — a highly specialized domain. The number of existing C++ implementations of this specific hardware is very small.
- The code is not implementing common algorithms or standard library functions where verbatim overlap with training data is most likely.
- Most of the codebase deals with hardware register emulation, raster timing, memory banking, and peripheral protocols — domains where the implementation is dictated by the hardware specification, not by creative coding choices.
- Modern LLMs (including Claude) are trained to minimize memorization of training data.

**b) Structural similarity risk: LOW-MODERATE**

- Some structural similarity to other ZX Spectrum emulators (FUSE, ZEsarUX, CSpect) is inevitable because they all implement the same hardware. This is **scènes à faire** — the legal doctrine that certain expressions are so standard in a given context that they cannot be copyrighted. When implementing a Z80 instruction decoder or ULA timing, there are only so many ways to do it correctly.
- The fact that the implementation is driven by an authoritative VHDL specification strengthens the scènes à faire argument: the code is dictated by the hardware, not by creative choice.

**c) The FUSE Z80 core — properly handled**

- The FUSE Z80 core is explicitly vendored in `third_party/fuse-z80/` with its original copyright notices intact and its GPLv2-or-later license file (`COPYING.GPL2`).
- It is not disguised, rewritten, or relicensed — it is openly attributed and used under its original license.
- GPLv2-or-later is forward-compatible with GPLv3, so including it in a GPLv3 project is legally sound.

### 3.3 License Compatibility in JNEXT

The project's license stack is clean:

| Component                  | License        | Compatible with GPLv3?                                        |
|----------------------------|----------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| JNEXT source               | GPLv3-or-later | — (this is the project license)                               |
| FUSE Z80 core              | GPLv2-or-later | **Yes** — "or later" clause allows use under GPLv3            |
| spdlog                     | MIT            | **Yes** — MIT is permissive, compatible with any GPL version  |
| fmt (spdlog dependency)    | MIT            | **Yes**                                                       |
| SDL2 (external dependency) | zlib           | **Yes** — zlib is permissive                                  |
| Qt 6 (external dependency) | LGPLv3         | **Yes** — LGPL is compatible with GPL when dynamically linked |

There are **no license conflicts** in the project's dependency tree. The GPLv3 license choice is legally sound with respect to all third-party components.

### 3.4 The "Binary-Only" Question

Releasing only binaries would be **counterproductive and impractical** for JNEXT:

1. **GPL-3.0 requires source code availability.** The FUSE Z80 core is GPLv2-or-later, which requires source distribution. The project's own GPLv3 license also requires it.
2. **Hiding source does not eliminate contamination risk.** Infringement (if any) exists at the moment of generation, not publication.
3. **The VHDL-driven development actually helps you.** Being able to point to the authoritative hardware specification as the source of your design decisions is a strength, not a vulnerability.
4. **It would undermine community trust.** An emulator project benefits enormously from open-source community engagement.

## 4. Answers to Your Specific Questions

### Q1: Who owns the copyright of the AI-generated code?

**Short answer:** You have a strong claim to copyright, given your level of creative involvement.

- **In the US (settled law after Thaler):** AI cannot be an author. If a human provides sufficient creative expression — design decisions, iterative refinement, integration — the human is the author. Your sustained direction of JNEXT's development across 9 phases, with architectural decisions documented in `EMULATOR-DESIGN-PLAN.md` and verified against VHDL hardware specifications, constitutes substantial creative involvement.

- **In the EU/Spain:** Similar — human intellectual creation is required. Your role as architect and director of the project, making all significant design and implementation decisions, supports your authorship claim.

- **Specific to JNEXT:** The emulator's design is driven by an authoritative hardware specification (VHDL source). Your creative contribution includes: choosing the accuracy model, designing the module architecture, deciding how to translate hardware signals into software abstractions, designing the debugging infrastructure, and building the Qt GUI. These are not trivial prompt-and-accept interactions — they represent sustained creative direction of a complex engineering project.

### Q2: What is the current state of opinion among developers, lawyers, and courts?

The landscape is fragmented:

- **Courts:** Human authorship required (Thaler). AI-assisted works *can* be copyrighted (US Copyright Office). AI memorization of training data = infringement (GEMA v. OpenAI, under appeal).
- **Lawyers:** Advise documenting human involvement and exercising caution. No consensus on the exact threshold of "sufficient involvement."
- **Free software community:** Divided. Linux kernel accepts AI-assisted code with attribution. QEMU/Gentoo ban it. FSF is concerned but has no definitive policy.
- **General sentiment:** Cautious pragmatism. Most developers use AI tools while aware of unresolved legal questions.

### Q3: Would there be any problem licensing JNEXT under GPL-3.0?

**No significant problems.** In fact, GPLv3 is arguably the most natural choice:

- The FUSE Z80 core (GPLv2-or-later) requires the combined work to be GPL-compatible. GPLv3 satisfies this.
- spdlog (MIT) and other dependencies are permissive and compatible.
- You have a reasonable claim to copyright over the AI-generated portions given your creative direction.
- **The only theoretical risk:** If your copyright claim is later challenged, some portions could be deemed public domain. This would not make your release illegal, but could weaken copyleft enforcement for those portions. Given the specialized nature of emulator code, this risk is very low in practice.

### Q4: Would it be prudent to release only in binary form?

**No, this would be counterproductive.** See Section 3.4 above. The FUSE Z80 core's GPL license requires source availability. Hiding source would violate the very licenses you've incorporated and would provide no meaningful legal protection.

### Q5: Would you be exposed to lawsuits if you released as GPLv3?

**The practical risk is very low for JNEXT specifically:**

- **Training data contamination claim:** Very unlikely. JNEXT implements highly specialized ZX Spectrum Next hardware. The code is driven by a VHDL specification, and the domain is so niche that verbatim reproduction of training data is improbable. No developer has been sued for using AI-generated code in their projects — lawsuits target AI providers, not end users.

- **Copyright challenge:** Someone could theoretically argue you don't hold valid copyright. But this wouldn't result in damages — at worst, some code would be public domain.

- **GPL compliance challenge:** If you properly comply with GPL terms (source availability, copyright notices, license text), this risk is negligible. Your current setup (LICENSE file, README attribution, vendored third-party code with original notices) is already solid.

- **Chardet-type "license laundering" accusation:** Not applicable. JNEXT is not a rewrite of an existing emulator to change its license. It is a new application, and the FUSE Z80 core retains its original license and attribution.

### Q6: Is there a consensus on training data contamination?

**No consensus, but JNEXT's risk profile is favorable:**

- The "contamination" concern is strongest for common, widely-replicated code patterns (web frameworks, utility functions, standard algorithms). JNEXT operates in a highly specialized domain — ZX Spectrum Next hardware emulation — where the training corpus of similar code is small.
- The implementation is driven by an authoritative specification (VHDL source), not by reproducing patterns from existing codebases. Where code must follow a specific pattern, this is because the hardware dictates it (scènes à faire), not because of contamination.
- The FUSE Z80 core — the most obvious overlap with existing emulator code — is properly vendored and licensed, not AI-generated.

### Q7: Does being based in Spain affect the situation?

**Spain/EU advantages for JNEXT:**

- EU AI Act transparency requirements (August 2026) will force AI providers to be clearer about training data, reducing contamination uncertainty over time.
- EU moral rights doctrine is strong in Spain — your rights as creative director are well-protected.
- AESIA is publishing compliance guidance.

**Spain/EU considerations:**

- The GEMA v. OpenAI ruling gives contamination claims stronger backing in Europe. However, that case concerned verbatim lyric reproduction, far removed from specialized emulator code.
- No Spanish court has addressed AI-generated code copyright specifically.

**Bottom line:** Being in Spain does not create additional risk for your situation. The EU's direction favors transparency from AI providers (which helps you) while potentially creating stricter rules that primarily affect the providers, not end users like you.

## 5. Conclusions and Recommendations

### Overall Assessment

JNEXT is in a **strong position** for GPL-3.0 publication. The project's characteristics — specialized domain, VHDL-driven implementation, properly attributed third-party code, substantial documented human direction — put it at the favorable end of the spectrum for AI-assisted works. The legal landscape is evolving, but nothing in current law prohibits or materially endangers what you are doing.

### Recommendations

#### 1. Publish under GPL-3.0 — your current setup is already solid

Your existing license infrastructure is good:
- `LICENSE` file with full GPLv3 text ✓
- Copyright notice (Copyright (C) 2025-2026 Jorge Gonzalez Villalonga) ✓
- `README.md` with GPL notice ✓
- FUSE Z80 core properly vendored with original copyright and `COPYING.GPL2` ✓
- spdlog as git submodule with MIT license intact ✓

The practical risk is low, and waiting for legal clarity will take years with no guarantee of a more favorable outcome.

#### 2. Document your creative involvement

You already have strong documentation in place:
- `EMULATOR-DESIGN-PLAN.md` — detailed 9-phase design plan with architectural decisions
- `FPGA-REPO-ANALYSIS.md` — demonstrates that the VHDL source is the authoritative specification
- `CLAUDE.md` — documents the development methodology
- Git history — shows iterative development over many sessions

**Recommended additions:**
- Add a brief note to the README or a `CONTRIBUTORS.md` file stating that the code was developed with the assistance of Claude Code (Anthropic), with you as the primary architect and creative director. Transparency builds trust and follows the Linux kernel model.
- Keep the `EMULATOR-DESIGN-PLAN.md`, git history, and daily prompt files intact — they are your strongest evidence of sustained human creative direction. The `.prompts/` directory contains dated daily prompt files (e.g., `2026-03-18.md` through `2026-03-22.md`) that serve as exhaustive traces of the creative process: the specific tasks requested, design decisions made, iteration feedback, and task completion status for each development session. These are particularly valuable as evidence of human authorship because they document the creative direction, not just the output.

#### 3. Add copyright headers to source files

Currently, the main source files in `src/` do not contain per-file copyright headers. While the project-level `LICENSE` file provides coverage, adding headers strengthens your position:

```cpp
// Copyright (C) 2025-2026 Jorge Gonzalez Villalonga
// SPDX-License-Identifier: GPL-3.0-or-later
```

The SPDX identifier is a standard, machine-readable format that makes license scanning unambiguous. This is a minor but worthwhile improvement.

#### 4. Review for obvious contamination — low effort, high value

Before publishing, do a lightweight review:
- Scan for any suspiciously specific code that looks like it might be from a well-known emulator (ZEsarUX, CSpect, FUSE beyond the vendored Z80 core).
- Look for leftover comments, variable names, or code patterns that seem copied from identifiable projects.
- Given the specialized domain and VHDL-driven development, the risk is very low, but a good-faith effort is prudent.

#### 5. Do NOT release binary-only

You cannot legally do this with GPLv2/GPLv3 third-party code. The GPL requires source code availability.

#### 6. Consider the EU AI Act labeling requirements

From August 2026, Article 50 may require labeling AI-generated or AI-assisted content. Your project would likely fall under "AI-assisted" (you directed development). Monitor the final guidelines (expected June 2026) and add appropriate labeling if required. A `CONTRIBUTORS.md` or README note about Claude Code assistance would likely satisfy this.

#### 7. Legal counsel is optional but available

For a personal/hobby project, the risk is low enough that formal legal counsel is not strictly necessary. However, if the project gains commercial significance or high visibility, organizations like the Software Freedom Law Center or FSFE can provide guidance.

## 6. Additional Questions of Interest

### 6.1 Anthropic's Terms of Service

Anthropic's terms of service for Claude should be reviewed to confirm you retain rights over generated output. Most AI providers (including Anthropic) explicitly grant users ownership of outputs. Verify this for your specific plan/tier.

### 6.2 The "Clean Room" Non-Issue for JNEXT

The chardet-style "license laundering" concern does not apply to JNEXT. You are not using AI to rewrite an existing emulator to change its license. You are building a new emulator from a hardware specification (VHDL source), and the one piece of existing emulator code you do use (FUSE Z80 core) retains its original license. If anything, your approach is the opposite of license laundering — you openly attribute and preserve original licenses.

### 6.3 GitHub Training Data Policy

GitHub announced (March 2026) that Copilot will use interaction data from Free, Pro, and Pro+ users to train AI models by default. Review your GitHub settings if you want to opt out before publishing the repository.

### 6.4 The Scènes à Faire Advantage

Emulator development has a natural legal advantage: much of the code is dictated by the hardware being emulated. A Z80 instruction decoder, ULA timing implementation, or NextREG register handler must work a specific way to be correct. This functional requirement limits the range of creative expression and strengthens a scènes à faire defense against contamination claims. The VHDL source acts as a functional specification that constrains the implementation space.

### 6.5 GPLv2-or-Later to GPLv3 Upgrade Path

The FUSE Z80 core is licensed as "GPLv2-or-later." By including it in a GPLv3 project, you are exercising the "or later" option. This is explicitly permitted by the GPL and is a well-established practice. No additional permission from the FUSE authors is needed.

### 6.6 Future-Proofing: The Evolving Definition of "Authorship"

The threshold for "sufficient human involvement" remains undefined. Your level of involvement — directing a complex multi-phase emulator project with documented architecture, VHDL cross-referencing, and iterative testing — is strong by any reasonable standard. Future court cases will eventually define the line more precisely, but you are well on the favorable side of wherever that line is likely to be drawn.
