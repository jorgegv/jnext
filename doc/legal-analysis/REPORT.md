## 1. Understanding of Your Situation and Concerns

You are developing a large application almost entirely through Claude Code, an AI coding assistant, with minimal direct code authorship on your part. The application is stored in a private GitHub repository and incorporates some existing GPL-3.0-licensed code from other repositories. You would like to publish the entire project under the GPL-3.0 license but are hesitant due to legal uncertainties.

Your core concerns are:

- **Copyright ownership**: Whether you can claim copyright over code that was predominantly generated by an AI tool, and what legal standing that code has.
- **License validity**: Whether you can meaningfully apply the GPL-3.0 license to AI-generated code, given that licensing presupposes copyright ownership.
- **Legal exposure**: Whether publishing the source code could expose you to lawsuits, either from original copyright holders whose code may have been ingested during AI training, or from others challenging your right to license the code.
- **Training data contamination**: Whether the code output by the LLM may be "contaminated" by copyrighted code the model was trained on, and what that means for you.
- **Jurisdictional concerns**: How your situation is affected by being based in Spain, within the EU, and how it differs from the US legal landscape.
- **Risk mitigation**: Whether releasing only binaries (rather than source code) would reduce your exposure while the legal landscape matures.

This is a prudent set of concerns. The legal landscape around AI-generated code is genuinely unsettled, and you are right to pause and assess before publishing.

## 2. Current State of Affairs

### 2.1 US Copyright Office Position

The US Copyright Office has established through multiple decisions and a formal report (Part 2, published January 29, 2025) that:

- **Human authorship is required** for copyright protection. Works generated solely by AI, without meaningful human creative input, are not eligible for copyright.
- **AI-assisted works can be copyrighted**, but only to the extent that a human author has contributed sufficient creative expression. The mere provision of prompts to an AI is generally not considered sufficient creative input.
- The use of AI to assist in creation, or the inclusion of AI-generated material in a larger human-generated work, does **not automatically bar** copyrightability.
- The Office recommends documenting human involvement in AI-assisted development.

### 2.2 Key Court Rulings

#### Thaler v. Perlmutter (Settled Law as of March 2026)

This is the landmark US case on AI authorship:

- **District Court (August 2023):** Ruled that AI cannot be an author under the Copyright Act.
- **D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals (March 2025):** Affirmed, stating that human authorship is a "bedrock requirement." Importantly, the court clarified: *"The human authorship requirement does not prohibit copyrighting work that was made by or with the assistance of artificial intelligence."* The author must be a human—the person who created, operated, or used the AI—not the machine itself.
- **US Supreme Court (March 2, 2026):** Denied certiorari, making this effectively settled law in the United States. AI cannot be an author, but humans using AI can be authors of the resulting works, depending on their level of creative involvement.

#### GEMA v. OpenAI (Munich, Germany — November 2025)

A European landmark:

- The Munich Regional Court ruled that OpenAI's GPT models **infringed copyright** by memorizing and reproducing song lyrics almost verbatim.
- The court held that **memorization of training data constitutes reproduction** under EU/German copyright law.
- The court rejected OpenAI's text and data mining (TDM) defense, holding that the TDM exception applies only to the initial analytical phase, not to the memorization and reproduction of works.
- OpenAI was ordered to cease, pay damages, and disclose revenue information.
- **This case is under appeal** and may eventually reach the Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU).
- **Significance for you:** This ruling establishes (in at least one EU jurisdiction) that if an AI model can reproduce copyrighted content from its training data, that constitutes infringement. This is directly relevant to the "contamination" concern.

#### Doe v. GitHub / GitHub Copilot Lawsuit (Ongoing)

- Filed November 2022 against GitHub, Microsoft, and OpenAI, alleging that Copilot was trained on open-source code and reproduces it without proper attribution or license compliance.
- Most claims (including DMCA claims) were dismissed over 2023-2024. A narrow set of claims (breach of contract theories) remain.
- The case has not produced a definitive ruling on whether AI-generated code constitutes a derivative work of training data.
- **As of early 2026, the lawsuit remains unresolved.**

### 2.3 EU Legal Framework

#### EU AI Act

- The EU AI Act entered into force and is being progressively implemented. Article 50 transparency obligations will apply from **August 2026**.
- The Act requires labeling of AI-generated content with a two-tier system: fully AI-generated vs. AI-assisted content.
- Spain's Agency for the Supervision of AI (AESIA) has published 16 guidance documents for compliance.

#### European Parliament Copyright and AI Report (2026)

- The European Parliament voted on a report on "Copyright and Generative AI" in January 2026, proposing:
  - Enhanced licensing and remuneration for training data use.
  - Broad territorial application: EU copyright law should apply to AI models placed on the EU market, **regardless of where training occurred**.
  - Greater transparency from AI providers about training data.
- This is not yet law but signals the direction of EU policy.

#### Spain-Specific Considerations

- Spain follows EU copyright law (transposed EU directives). Spanish copyright law (Ley de Propiedad Intelectual, Real Decreto Legislativo 1/1996) requires human authorship for copyright protection, consistent with the general EU position.
- AESIA is actively publishing AI compliance guidance.
- No Spain-specific court ruling on AI-generated code copyright exists yet, but EU-level developments (GEMA v. OpenAI, EP reports) will shape the legal landscape.

### 2.4 International Perspectives

- **Japan:** Has historically been more permissive regarding AI and copyright. Japan's copyright law allows certain AI training uses without permission, though this is under ongoing debate.
- **UK:** The UK previously had a provision (CDPA 1988, Section 9(3)) granting copyright for computer-generated works to "the person by whom the arrangements necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken." This provision is unique globally but has not been tested with modern generative AI.
- **General trend:** Most jurisdictions are converging on requiring human authorship for copyright, while the specific thresholds of "how much human involvement" remain undefined.

### 2.5 Open Source Community Positions

#### Free Software Foundation (FSF)

- The FSF has expressed concern that LLMs trained on GPL-licensed code could **inadvertently violate copyleft provisions**.
- They advocate for AI transparency and argue that software freedom principles should extend to AI systems.
- The FSF has funded research papers on AI code copilots and continues to develop its position.
- **No definitive policy statement** has been issued specifically about whether individual developers can GPL-license AI-generated code.

#### Linux Kernel Policy

The Linux kernel has adopted the most detailed policy of any major project:

- AI tools must adhere to all existing kernel development processes and coding standards.
- **AI agents MUST NOT add Signed-off-by tags.** Only humans can certify the Developer Certificate of Origin (DCO).
- Contributions assisted by AI must include an `Assisted-by:` tag (e.g., `Assisted-by: Claude:claude-3-opus`).
- The human developer bears **full responsibility** for reviewing AI-generated code, verifying licensing compliance, and taking accountability for the submission.
- Linus Torvalds has stated that AI tools should be treated no differently than traditional coding aids.

#### Other Projects

- **QEMU and Gentoo** have banned AI-generated contributions, citing copyright uncertainty, quality concerns, and DCO incompatibility.
- **Debian** debated AI contributions but reached no formal decision.
- There is no universal community consensus.

### 2.6 The "Chardet" License Laundering Incident (March 2026)

A highly relevant and very recent case:

- The maintainer of `chardet` (a Python character encoding library) used Claude to rewrite the entire codebase and changed the license from LGPL to MIT, claiming it was a "clean room implementation."
- The original author, Mark Pilgrim, objected, calling it an explicit LGPL violation.
- The open-source community widely criticized this as **"license laundering"** — using AI to circumvent copyleft obligations.
- The core question: if an AI rewrites code with the same functionality but different expression, is it a derivative work?
- **No court has ruled on this**, but the incident demonstrates the real-world risks and community sensitivity around AI-generated code and licensing.

## 3. Detailed Analysis

### 3.1 The Copyright Paradox for AI-Generated Code

There is a fundamental tension at the heart of your situation:

1. **To license code (under GPL or any license), you need to hold copyright over it.** The GPL-3.0 is a copyright license — it grants permissions that only the copyright holder can grant.

2. **AI-generated code may not be copyrightable** if the human's contribution was insufficient. Under both US and EU law, purely AI-generated content likely falls into the public domain.

3. **If the code is in the public domain, you cannot effectively license it under GPL-3.0**, because there is nothing to license. Anyone could take the code and use it without restriction, defeating the purpose of copyleft.

However, the reality is nuanced:

- The "spectrum of human involvement" matters. On one end: typing a single prompt and accepting the output verbatim (likely not copyrightable by you). On the other: iteratively directing the AI, reviewing output, making creative decisions about architecture and design, editing and refining code, integrating components (likely copyrightable, at least in part).

- **Your situation** — developing a "big application" through Claude Code — likely involves substantial creative direction: deciding what features to build, how to architect the system, reviewing and accepting or rejecting suggestions, integrating with existing GPL code, testing and iterating. This puts you in a stronger position than someone who blindly accepts AI output.

### 3.2 The Contamination Risk

The "contamination" argument has two distinct forms:

**a) Verbatim reproduction:** The AI reproduces recognizable chunks of copyrighted code from its training data. This is the most legally clear-cut risk. The GEMA v. OpenAI ruling confirms (in Germany) that this can constitute copyright infringement. Research has shown that code-generating AIs can sometimes reproduce verbatim snippets, including license headers.

**b) Structural similarity:** The AI produces code that, while not verbatim, is substantially similar to specific copyrighted code it was trained on. This is harder to detect and legally murkier.

**Practical risk assessment:** Modern LLMs (including Claude) are trained with techniques to reduce memorization and verbatim reproduction. The risk of verbatim reproduction of large code blocks is low but non-zero, especially for very popular or distinctive code patterns. The risk of inadvertent structural similarity is higher but harder to prosecute.

### 3.3 The GPL-Specific Complication

Your application already contains GPL-3.0 code from other repositories. This creates an additional layer:

- The existing GPL-3.0 code **is** copyrighted by its original authors and **does** carry a valid license.
- The GPL-3.0 requires that derivative works also be distributed under GPL-3.0.
- If you're combining this GPL code with AI-generated code in a single program, the GPL's copyleft provision would require the entire combined work to be GPL-3.0.
- For the AI-generated portions: if they are copyrightable (because of your creative involvement), you can license them under GPL-3.0. If they are not copyrightable (public domain), they can still be *included* in a GPL-3.0 project — public domain code can be incorporated into any project — but others could theoretically extract those portions and use them without GPL restrictions.

### 3.4 The "Binary-Only" Question

Releasing only binaries does not eliminate legal risk; it only changes its character:

- If AI-generated code contains memorized copyrighted snippets, the infringement exists at the moment of reproduction (when the AI generated it and when you compiled it), not only when source code is published.
- Binary distribution still requires license compliance for the GPL components you incorporated.
- The GPL-3.0 itself requires that you make source code available to recipients of the binary. Distributing binaries under GPL-3.0 without providing source code would violate the GPL terms of the third-party GPL code you incorporated.
- Hiding source code merely delays discovery; it does not eliminate the underlying legal issue.

## 4. Answers to Your Specific Questions

### Q1: Who owns the copyright of the AI-generated code?

**Short answer:** It depends on the level of human creative involvement.

- **In the US (settled law after Thaler v. Perlmutter, affirmed by Supreme Court denial of certiorari, March 2026):** AI itself cannot be an author or copyright holder. If a human provides sufficient creative expression — through design decisions, iterative refinement, editing, and integration — the human is the author. If the AI's output is used with minimal human creative input, it may not be copyrightable at all and would effectively be in the public domain.

- **In the EU/Spain:** The situation is similar. EU copyright law requires human intellectual creation. The EU has no equivalent to the UK's "computer-generated works" provision. Works generated purely by AI without meaningful human creative contribution are likely not copyrightable.

- **For your situation specifically:** Given that you are directing the development of a full application (making architectural decisions, choosing features, reviewing and iterating on output, integrating with existing code), you have a reasonable argument that you are the author. However, the exact threshold of "sufficient creative involvement" when using AI tools like Claude Code has not been tested in court.

### Q2: What is the current state of opinion among free software developers, lawyers, and courts?

The landscape is fragmented:

- **Courts:** Agree that human authorship is required (Thaler v. Perlmutter). Agree that AI-assisted works *can* be copyrighted (US Copyright Office Part 2 report). The GEMA v. OpenAI ruling in Germany found that AI memorization of training data constitutes copyright infringement.

- **Lawyers:** Generally advise documenting human involvement, being cautious about claiming copyright over minimally-modified AI output, and watching the evolving case law. There is no consensus on exactly where the line falls for "sufficient human involvement."

- **Free software community:** Divided. The Linux kernel accepts AI-assisted contributions with attribution, placing full responsibility on the human developer. QEMU and Gentoo ban them. The FSF is concerned about copyleft erosion but has not issued a definitive policy. The chardet incident (March 2026) has inflamed community concerns about "license laundering."

- **General sentiment:** Cautious pragmatism. Most developers and organizations are using AI tools but are aware of the unresolved legal questions.

### Q3: Would there be any problem licensing my code under GPL-3.0?

**Practical answer:** You can do it, but with caveats.

- The existing GPL-3.0 code from other repositories retains its license. No issue there.
- For the AI-generated portions: if your creative involvement is sufficient (which, as the director and reviewer of the entire application, it plausibly is), you can claim copyright and license under GPL-3.0.
- **The risk:** If your copyright claim over the AI-generated portions is later challenged and found insufficient, those portions could be deemed public domain. This would not make your release *illegal*, but it could mean the copyleft provisions of GPL-3.0 are unenforceable for those portions. Someone could extract the AI-generated parts and use them without GPL restrictions.
- **The contamination risk:** If Claude reproduced copyrighted code from its training data in your project, the original copyright holder could (in theory) object. This risk exists regardless of what license you choose.

### Q4: Would it be prudent to release only in binary form?

**No, this would actually be counterproductive for several reasons:**

1. **GPL-3.0 requires source code availability.** Since your application contains GPL-3.0 code from other repositories, you are legally obligated under those licenses to provide source code to anyone who receives the binary. Releasing only binaries would violate the GPL terms of the code you incorporated.

2. **Hiding source code does not eliminate contamination risk.** If the AI produced infringing code, the infringement exists regardless of whether you publish the source.

3. **Transparency is your ally.** Publishing source code allows the community to review it and flag potential issues. This is better than having an infringement silently embedded in your binary.

4. **It would undermine your credibility.** Releasing a supposedly GPL-3.0 project without source code is a GPL violation and would damage trust.

### Q5: Would you be exposed to lawsuits if you released as GPLv3?

**The honest answer is: yes, there is some theoretical exposure, but the practical risk is low.**

Possible (but unlikely) lawsuit scenarios:

- **Copyright holder whose code was memorized by the AI:** If Claude reproduced recognizable chunks of someone's copyrighted code in your project, that person could theoretically sue for infringement. The GEMA v. OpenAI ruling supports this theory in Europe. However: (a) modern LLMs are designed to minimize this, (b) the plaintiff would need to discover the similarity and prove it, (c) the more likely target would be Anthropic (the AI provider) rather than you as the end user.

- **Challenge to your copyright claim:** Someone could argue that you don't hold valid copyright over the AI-generated portions, undermining your GPL license. But this wouldn't result in *damages* against you — at worst, the code would be treated as public domain.

- **GPL compliance challenge:** If you don't properly comply with the GPL-3.0 terms for the third-party code you incorporated (e.g., not providing source, not including proper notices), the original authors could enforce their GPL rights.

**What is NOT a realistic risk:**

- Being sued simply for publishing AI-generated code under GPL-3.0 is not something any current legal theory supports as a cause of action.
- The GitHub Copilot lawsuit targets the AI providers, not individual developers using the tools.

### Q6: Is there a consensus on training data contamination?

**There is no consensus, but there are emerging positions:**

- **The "contamination" camp:** Argues that since LLMs are trained on vast amounts of copyrighted code (under various licenses), their outputs are inherently suspect and may constitute unlicensed derivative works. The FSF leans toward this view. The GEMA v. OpenAI ruling partially validates this position (at least for verbatim or near-verbatim reproduction).

- **The "tool" camp:** Argues that LLMs are tools, like compilers or IDEs, and their output should be treated like any other code written by the human user. Linus Torvalds and many pragmatic developers hold this view. The US Copyright Office's position (that AI-assisted works can be copyrighted) implicitly supports this.

- **The middle ground (majority view):** Most legal experts and organizations acknowledge the risk is real but context-dependent. The risk is highest when: (a) the AI reproduces verbatim or near-verbatim code, (b) the training data source is identifiable, (c) the original code has distinctive creative expression. The risk is lowest when: the output is generic, functional code that could be written many ways.

- **Practical reality:** No developer has been sued for using AI-generated code in their projects. The lawsuits target AI providers (Microsoft, GitHub, OpenAI), not end users. This may change, but it is the current state.

### Q7: Does being based in Spain affect the situation?

**Yes, your jurisdiction matters:**

**Spain/EU advantages:**

- The EU AI Act will require transparency from AI providers about training data (effective August 2026), which may eventually give you better tools to assess contamination risk.
- The GEMA v. OpenAI ruling, if upheld and adopted more broadly, could force AI providers to clean up training data practices, reducing contamination risk over time.
- EU copyright's "moral rights" doctrine (strong in Spain) means you retain certain rights as the creative director of the work, even beyond economic copyright.
- Spain's AESIA is actively publishing AI compliance guidance.

**Spain/EU complications:**

- The GEMA v. OpenAI ruling establishes (in Germany, persuasive in the EU) that memorized training data = reproduction. This means the contamination theory has *more* legal support in Europe than in the US.
- The European Parliament's proposed territorial expansion of copyright law to AI models "placed on the Union market" could create additional obligations.
- No Spanish court has specifically addressed AI-generated code copyright.

**Compared to the US:**

- In the US, the Copilot lawsuit's narrow survival suggests it is difficult (though not impossible) to win copyright claims against AI code generators.
- The US has a stronger "fair use" doctrine, which may protect AI training more than the EU's narrower TDM exceptions.
- The US Copyright Office has been more explicit about when AI-assisted works *can* be copyrighted.

**Bottom line for your jurisdiction:** Being in Spain means you operate under EU copyright law, which is trending toward stricter regulation of AI and stronger protection for training data copyright holders. This cuts both ways: it may make it harder for others to challenge your use of AI tools (because the burden falls on AI providers), but it also means contamination claims have stronger legal backing if someone discovers their code in your output.

## 5. Conclusions and Recommendations

### Overall Assessment

Your situation is legally navigable but requires thoughtful action. The law is evolving rapidly, and no single court ruling definitively resolves all your questions. However, the balance of current law, policy, and practical risk supports the following approach:

### Recommendations

#### 1. Go ahead and publish under GPL-3.0, but with proper preparation

The practical risk of lawsuits is low, and the legal landscape — while unsettled — does not prohibit what you are doing. The GPL-3.0 third-party code you've incorporated *requires* you to distribute under GPL-3.0 anyway. Not publishing over legal uncertainty is a valid choice, but the uncertainty is unlikely to resolve definitively for years. Waiting gains little.

#### 2. Document your creative involvement thoroughly

This is the single most important step you can take to protect yourself:

- Keep records of your design decisions, architectural choices, and creative direction.
- Document your review and iteration process — the prompts you gave, the output you rejected, the changes you made.
- Be able to demonstrate that you exercised creative judgment, not just pressed a button.
- This documentation is your evidence of human authorship if ever challenged.

#### 3. Add clear attribution and transparency

- In your repository, include a note (e.g., in the README or a CONTRIBUTORS file) indicating that the code was developed with the assistance of Claude Code (Anthropic).
- Follow the Linux kernel's model: attribute AI assistance transparently. This builds trust and demonstrates good faith.
- Properly attribute all third-party GPL-3.0 code you incorporated, with copyright notices intact.

#### 4. Review the code for obvious contamination

- Before publishing, do a review (manual or automated) for any suspiciously specific code that looks like it might have been reproduced from a well-known library.
- Look for leftover comments, variable names, or code patterns that seem copied from identifiable projects.
- Tools for code similarity detection (e.g., MOSS, JPlag, or commercial alternatives) can help.
- This does not need to be exhaustive, but a good-faith effort reduces risk significantly.

#### 5. Do NOT release binary-only

- You cannot legally do this with GPL-3.0 third-party code anyway — the GPL requires source code availability.
- Transparency (publishing source) is actually a protective measure, not a vulnerability.

#### 6. Include proper GPL-3.0 compliance

- Include the full GPL-3.0 license text.
- Include copyright notices for all third-party GPL code.
- For your own code, you can use a copyright notice such as: `Copyright (C) 2026 Jorge G.V.` — this asserts your claim as the creative author/director of the work.
- Include a NOTICE or similar file explaining the project's provenance.

#### 7. Consider obtaining legal counsel

- If the application has commercial significance or you anticipate significant public visibility, consider consulting a lawyer specializing in intellectual property and software licensing in Spain/EU.
- Organizations like the Software Freedom Law Center or the FSFE may be able to provide guidance.

## 6. Additional Questions of Interest

During this research, several additional questions emerged that may be relevant to you:

### 6.1 AI Provider Terms of Service

Anthropic's terms of service for Claude should be reviewed to confirm that you retain rights over code generated through Claude Code. Most AI providers (including Anthropic) explicitly state that users retain ownership of outputs. Verify this for your specific plan/tier.

### 6.2 The "Clean Room" Problem

The chardet incident (March 2026) raises a critical question: can AI be used for clean-room implementations? The traditional clean-room process requires that the implementer has **no access** to the original code. Since LLMs were trained on vast amounts of code, they inherently "have access" to the original. This may invalidate clean-room claims for AI-generated code. This is relevant if any part of your project was prompted to "implement something like X" where X is an existing library.

### 6.3 GitHub's Training Data Policy Change

GitHub announced (March 2026) that Copilot will use interaction data from Free, Pro, and Pro+ users to train AI models **by default**. This means your interactions with GitHub (though not necessarily Claude Code) could feed back into AI training. Enterprise accounts are exempt. Review your GitHub tier and settings.

### 6.4 Future EU Labeling Requirements

From August 2026, the EU AI Act's Article 50 will require labeling AI-generated content. The Code of Practice distinguishes between "fully AI-generated" and "AI-assisted" content. Your application would likely fall under "AI-assisted" (since you directed the development), but you should monitor this requirement as final guidelines are expected in June 2026.

### 6.5 Insurance and Indemnification

Some AI providers offer indemnification for IP claims related to their AI's output (e.g., Microsoft offers this for Copilot Enterprise users). Check whether Anthropic offers any similar protection for Claude Code users. This could be an additional risk mitigation measure.

### 6.6 The Evolving Definition of "Authorship"

The threshold for "sufficient human involvement" to claim authorship of AI-assisted works remains undefined. The US Copyright Office says prompting alone is not enough, but iterative design, review, and integration likely is. The exact line will be drawn by future court cases. Your level of involvement (directing a full application) is on the stronger side of this spectrum, but there are no guarantees.
